## The Email

Hi [Reporter's Name],

Here are answers 4 and 5:

**4. Decision-Making Differences**

Every hire we made in the early years had to pass one test: will this person generate positive ROI within the first few months? Not "maybe" - we needed high confidence that the hire would cover their own cost quickly. That was the rule.

If I had raised money, I probably would have taken bigger swings - hired researchers, explored unproven markets, tested things I wasn't sure about. With our own money, we couldn't afford to guess. So we looked at what competitors were already doing successfully, confirmed there was demand, and then hired someone to do it better and scale it.

A concrete example: before we hired a PPC specialist, I ran the ad campaigns myself first. Proved the channel worked, proved the ROI was there. Only then did we bring someone in to scale it. Every hire was like that - validate first, hire second.

**5. The Trade-offs**

The biggest one: I couldn't pay market-rate salaries in the early days. That meant I couldn't bring in top-tier professionals from day one. It hurt in some ways - we had to replace people along the way, and sometimes we moved slower because I needed to develop talent internally instead of hiring someone who already had the skills.

But it also created something I didn't expect. Because I invested in teaching and growing people, loyalty was incredibly high. Retention was above 90%. People saw the mission, they grew with the company, and they stayed.

More broadly, bootstrapping forces a healthier business. You can't spray money and hope something sticks. You laser-focus on specific goals, and when something works, you go all in. No guessing.

That discipline proved itself after COVID, when the market shifted and investors suddenly wanted profitable companies. We already were one. We weren't a company burning cash and hoping for the next round - we had a real business with real margins.

Best,
Lior

## Copywriter Notes

### Choices I Made
- **No greeting fluff** - this is a continuation email, he already knows who I am. Straight to the answers.
- **Q4 structure:** Rule > what-if contrast > concrete example. The PPC example is specific and quotable.
- **Q5 structure:** Pain > unexpected upside > broader principle > COVID proof point. Balanced "it hurt AND it helped" as the journalist asked.
- **Applied new owner directives:** No standalone punch lines as guru pronouncements. "That discipline proved itself" flows into the paragraph, not isolated. No superiority framing - "I couldn't afford" is honest, not "I was smarter than VC-backed founders."
- **Eye-level tone throughout.** Sharing what happened, not lecturing about what others should do.
- **Straight quotes only** per owner feedback.
- **Signed "Lior" not "Lior Pozin, CEO"** - this is a second email in the thread, first-name is natural.
- **Kept "we" for company decisions** (hiring, scaling) and "I" for personal actions (running campaigns, teaching people).
