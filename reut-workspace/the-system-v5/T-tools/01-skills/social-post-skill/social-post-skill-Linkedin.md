# LinkedIn Post Skill

Create LinkedIn posts that stop the scroll and start conversations. LinkedIn-specific.

## When to Use

- Writing LinkedIn posts (text, carousel ideas, article hooks)
- Converting ideas into LinkedIn-optimized content
- Creating thought leadership content for a professional audience
- Need a strong hook to open a LinkedIn post

## LinkedIn Post Basics

**What works on LinkedIn:**
- Personal stories with professional insights
- Contrarian takes with evidence
- Practical frameworks people can use immediately
- Vulnerability that teaches
- Transformation stories (before/after)

text length- 
A post should be as long as it needs to be to provide value, but as short as possible to respect the reader's time.

- **The Hook (1 Line):** The most important part. If you don't stop the scroll here, the rest doesn't exist.
    
- **The Bridge (2-3 Lines):** Connect the hook to a personal story or a pain point that readers can relate to.
    
- **The Body (The Meat):** Use listicles or short, punchy paragraphs. No walls of text.
    
- **The Insight:** One clear, non-obvious takeaway.
    
- **The CTA (Call to Action):** A specific question to trigger the algorithm through comments.
    

## 2. Character Count Strategy

|**Post Type**|**Length (Characters)**|**Why it works**|
|---|---|---|
|**The Power Opinion**|400 – 700|Short, bold, and triggers debates. High "Dwell Time" because people re-read it.|
|**The Story + Lesson**|1,200 – 1,600|This is the **Sweet Spot**. Enough space for a narrative (e.g., the VAT crisis) and 5 actionable tips.|
|**The Authority Guide**|1,800 – 2,500|Rare. Used for deep-dive frameworks or massive milestones. Great for "Save" rates.|

## 3. The "Anti-AI" Writing Rules

To make an agent sound human and authoritative, it must follow these constraints:

- **No Fluff:** Remove "In today’s fast-paced world" or "Unlock your potential."
    
- **One Sentence Paragraphs:** This is built for mobile reading.
    
- **Zero Double Dashes:** Use periods or line breaks instead.
    
- **Sparse Emojis:** Never use the rocket 🚀. It looks like spam.
    
- **Direct Tone:** Don't say "I think." Say "Here is how it's done."
    

The goal - build authority from someone who actually runs a company.


**LinkedIn-specific rules:**
- Put links in the first comment (better reach)
- 3-5 hashtags max
- Professional tone with personal stories
- Thought leadership and industry insights work best
- 57% of LinkedIn traffic is from mobile. Short hooks matter.

## The 8 Principles of a Killer LinkedIn Hook

The hook is EVERYTHING. Most people only see the first 2 lines before they decide to keep reading or scroll past.

Write your hook AFTER you write your content. Ask yourself: "What's the biggest benefit the reader gets out of this post?"

### Principle 1: Focus on a Benefit or Problem

Readers want content that solves their problems or helps them get what they want. Give them a reason to read.

Ask yourself: "What do they get out of this post?"

```
Don't: "Magical SEO secrets"
(vague, no real benefit)

Do: "5 ways to rank 1st on google"
(clear benefit the audience wants)
```

### Principle 2: Show a Transformation

Sharing a story of transformation piques readers' curiosity. They'll click "see more" because they want to know how to get the same results. The bigger the difference, the better the hook.

**Template:**
```
[x time ago] I had [worse result]
Now I have [better result] - here's how:
```

**Examples:**
```
A year ago I didn't use LinkedIn.
Last month I got 201 leads on LinkedIn - here's how:
```
```
3 months ago I got 5k LinkedIn impressions per week.
Now I get 60k impressions per week - here's how:
```

### Principle 3: Add Ease of Use

Humans are lazy. We want it fast, easy, and now. The more you make your solution seem easy, the more people will click "see more".

```
"5 SIMPLE ways to rank 1st on google"
```

The word "simple" makes your solution seem easier. Also try: Easy, Fast.

Use "simple" as default. Use "fast" and "easy" when appropriate.

### Principle 4: Use Numbers

Our eyes are drawn to numbers, especially on platforms with a lot of text.

**Numbers help with:**
- Ease of use ("4 simple steps to X" > "how to do X")
- Pattern interrupting (catches attention in a sea of text)
- Credibility (when using stats)

### Principle 5: Be Polarising

People love engaging with content they strongly agree or disagree with. Content is like wrestling: boos or cheers are always better than silence.

Be honest. Be authoritative. Share your true thoughts. Make strong statements. Don't hedge your bets.

```
Don't: "AI will replace some social media managers"
(weak statement people can ignore)

Do: "AI will replace social media managers"
(strong statement that pushes discussion from both sides)
```

### Principle 6: Keep It Short

A short hook grabs attention faster. Long hooks are cut off on mobile. Get to the point and keep it brief. Less is more.

### Principle 7: Be Specific

Specificity builds trust and attracts the right audience.

```
Don't: "about 60% of LinkedIn traffic is from mobile"
Do: "57% of LinkedIn traffic is from mobile"

Don't: "80k followers"
Do: "81,593 followers"
```

You can also use specificity to attract the right readers:

```
General: "5 ways to rank 1st on google"
Specific: "5 ways to rank 1st on google for SaaS companies"
```

### Principle 8: Be Clear

Clear beats clever every time. The more you make them think, the faster they leave. If people need to figure out what you mean, they're not going to read your post.

Keep it simple. Tell them exactly what they're going to get while also making them curious.

**Clear + curious = great hooks.**

## Writing Rules for LinkedIn

### Rule 1: One Idea Per Post
Don't try to say everything. One clear message, well delivered.

### Rule 2: Short Lines
```
Not this:
"I realized that after years of trying to optimize my productivity by working longer hours, the real answer was actually working fewer hours but with more focus."

This:
I worked longer hours.
Productivity went down.

I worked fewer hours with more focus.
Everything changed.
```

### Rule 3: White Space
Break up your text.

Single sentences get attention.

Paragraphs get skimmed.

### Rule 4: Specificity
```
Bad: "I improved my results significantly"
Good: "My close rate went from 12% to 34%"
```

### Rule 5: End With Engagement
Ask a question. Invite a response. Make it easy to comment.

```
Good endings:
- "What would you add to this list?"
- "Have you experienced this? Share below."
- "Agree or disagree?"
- "What's worked for you?"
```

## What to Avoid on LinkedIn

1. **Starting with "I'm excited to announce..."** - No one cares about your excitement
2. **Walls of text** - Break it up
3. **Vague claims** - "Best" means nothing without proof
4. **Multiple CTAs** - One ask per post
5. **Hashtag stuffing** - 3-5 max
6. **Clever over clear** - If they need to think about what you mean, they'll scroll past
7. **Weak statements** - Don't hedge. Make strong claims and back them up
8. **Long hooks** - Mobile cuts them off. Get to the point

How to end LinkedIn post to create engagement- use 1 of these tactics- 
- **Ask a Question:** Encourage comments by asking clever question that let people start a discussion. for example: "What do you think - are we heading toward a world where engineers don't write code at all?" or "מה דעתכם? השקעה של קרן Tier-1 באמת מגדילה את סיכויי ההצלחה, או שהיא בעיקר מייצרת לחץ מיותר ודילול גבוה?"
- **Summarize (TL;DR):** Offer a one-sentence takeaway for busy professionals scrolling through their feed.
- **Use Connectors:** Phrases like "To sum up," "Remember," or "Don't forget" can tie your conclusion to the main body of the post.
