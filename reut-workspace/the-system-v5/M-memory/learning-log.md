## Active Patterns (Apply These Now)

### Copy Patterns

**What Works:**
| Do This | Not This |
|---------|----------|
| Start with a specific moment or action | Start with a generic statement |
| Use contrast frames: "Old way... New way..." | Use abstract explanations |
| Include numbers that can be visualized | Use vague claims |
| Write short lines. Punchy rhythm. | Write long flowing paragraphs |

**Voice Markers (Must Be Present):**
- Personal pronouns (I, my, me, you)
- Specific numbers (20 hours → 7 hours)
- Sensory/visual details
- Conversational tone

**Voice Violations (Must Be Absent):**
- Corporate jargon (leverage, optimize, synergy)
- Vague superlatives (best, leading, top-tier)
- Generic claims that competitors could use
- Passive voice overuse
- Long explanatory sentences

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Copy Mistakes
1. Opening with a lesson instead of a moment
2. Using "best" or "leading" without proof
3. Writing headlines that could apply to any competitor
4. Over-explaining the insight (let it land)
5. Missing the natural brand callback

---

## Quality Shortcuts

**Copy Quick Checks:**
1. Read it aloud. Does it flow?
2. Count specific numbers. Aim for 2+ per piece.
3. Check first line. Is it a moment or an explanation?
4. Check last line. Is it memorable standalone?
5. Apply the test: Visual? Provable? Unique?
6. **Content Quality Test (Lior's Rule):** Would I share this with a friend? Why would they follow you?

**4. Apply the Lior Test harder: "Could only Lior say this?"**
The IKEA/Punch analysis could have been written by any marketing account. Nothing in the post required Lior's specific experience. The WHOOP post passed because the guerrilla marketing angle aligned with Lior's competitive, rule-breaking identity.

### Critical Lessons (NEW RULES)

**1. EMPATHY HOOK > TRANSFORMATION HOOK for teaching posts.**
System defaulted to "look what I have" (transformation). Owner chose "you know this pain" (empathy). When the post teaches a personal system, start with the reader's frustration, not your result. The result comes AFTER they're hooked by recognition.

**2. Actionable framework > Descriptive framework.**
System described what's inside Lior's prompt (sections, content). Owner gave 3 steps the reader can execute immediately. Rule: when teaching a system, give the HOW-TO, not the WHAT'S-INSIDE. The reader doesn't care what Lior's prompt contains. They care how to build their own.

**3. "Ask GPT to provide everything it learned about you" is a paradigm shift.**
The system assumed the reader has to write their prompt from scratch. Owner's Step 1 uses AI to generate the raw material ABOUT you, then you organize it. This is Lior's actual insight and the system missed it completely. Lesson: when Lior shares a process, ask HOW he actually does it, don't assume.

**4. Owner cut ALL signposting and proof.**
No "3 companies," no "250+ employees," no "investor emails." The post is 100% teaching. Zero authority flex. This contradicts the LinkedIn skill's signposting rule. New nuance: **signposting is optional when the content itself demonstrates authority.** The framework IS the proof. Confirmed pattern (also seen in Post 09 OpenClaw).

**5. Owner compressed aggressively.**
System v3: ~1,450 characters. Owner final: ~750 characters. Owner cut the post nearly in HALF. The entire middle section (proof, specifics, social proof) was removed. Lesson: when the insight is simple, the post should be short. Don't pad with proof when the idea speaks for itself.

**6. "What I'll never say" > "What I never say."**
Future tense ("I'll never") is a permanent commitment. Present tense ("I never") is just a current habit. Subtle but more powerful. Track this pattern.

**7. "One prompt that saves me hours of work."**
Owner added this line. System never quantified the BENEFIT. This is the WIFM (What's In It For Me) the reader needs. Rule: always state the concrete benefit before teaching the system.

**8. "Living document" framing.**
Owner's Step 3 introduces maintenance: "as you evolve, your prompt needs to evolve too." System treated the prompt as a one-time creation. Owner sees it as ongoing. This adds depth and realism.

### Owner's Editing Instinct (Post 10 Pattern)
- Starts with reader pain, not writer achievement
- Cuts all proof/signposting when the teaching IS the proof
- Compresses 1,450 chars to 750 chars (nearly 50% cut)
- Changes descriptive frameworks to actionable how-to steps
- Adds the key insight the system missed (use AI to analyze yourself)
- Keeps the CTA clean and unchanged

## 2026-02-15 - Communication-Level Style Analysis: 8 Content Samples Deep Pass

### Analysis Conducted
**Status:** Complete — Re-analyzed all 8 content samples (sample-01 through sample-08) with focus on micro-level communication patterns not captured in previous analyses.

### New Patterns Discovered (Not Previously Documented)

**1. Punctuation Fingerprint**
Lior has a specific, consistent punctuation style that AI drafts get wrong:
- Single dash with space (` - `) for parenthetical: "trust, and social proof - gone in one algorithm update"
- Dash-without-space before pivots: "This wasn't a mistake- WHOOP knew the rules" / "Instead-"
- Colon as reveal device: "Their message was clear:" / "My response was simple:"
- NEVER em dash, NEVER double dash
- **Why this matters:** Getting punctuation wrong is the #1 tell that copy is AI-generated. Lior's dash style is unique and must be replicated exactly.

**2. "It" as Forward-Pull Device**
- Sentences starting with vague "It" create momentum: "It wasn't cheap" / "It's just choosing to pay" / "It was ugly. But it worked."
- Reader needs the next line to resolve the ambiguity — creates natural pull
- Frequency: 1-2 per post
- **Why this matters:** This is a subtle readability technique that AI rarely uses. Most AI writes explicit subjects.

**3. Casual Profanity as Emphasis**
- "Hell no." (Nike post) and "avoid the BS" — controlled, rare, purposeful
- Never vulgar or aggressive — just real
- Max 1 per post, only for strong conviction moments
- **Why this matters:** AI defaults to clean language. Occasional profanity is a Lior marker.

**4. Dialogue as Load-Bearing Structure**
- Previous analysis noted "quoted reactions" but missed that dialogue is STRUCTURAL — it carries the narrative, not just decorates it
- Coffee shop post: entire post pivots on one question: "How many customers have paid for this?"
- Shopify post: quoted reaction IS the lesson: "Doesn't matter. Let's focus on what's next."
- Hebrew post: someone else's words as the hook
- **Why this matters:** AI tends to use quotes for color. Lior uses them as the spine of the post.

**5. Paragraph Compression Evolution**
- Earlier samples (01-06): extreme white space, 1 idea per line
- Later samples (07-08): tighter, denser paragraphs — especially in numbered teaching sections
- Sample 07's numbered steps are each full inline paragraphs, not bullet fragments
- Matches owner editing feedback ("prefers tighter flow")
- **Why this matters:** The system was producing too much white space. Lior's evolving preference is denser flow, especially when teaching.

**6. Hebrew Emotional Directness**
- Hebrew posts use explicit emotional vocabulary: "האגו שלי קצת נפגע" (my ego was a bit hurt), "נעלבתי" (I was offended)
- English posts show emotion through scene and action (not stated feelings)
- This is a register difference, not just language — Hebrew Lior is more emotionally transparent
- **Why this matters:** When writing Hebrew content, lean into stated emotions. When writing English, show through scenes.

**7. Pronoun Precision (Expanded)**
- Previous analysis noted "I" vs "we" but missed the "the team" separation pattern
- "when the team showed me the update" — Lior separates himself from the org even though it's his company
- This creates a CEO-as-narrator distance that makes the content feel like leadership perspective
- **Why this matters:** Using "we showed ourselves" would sound weird. The separation is intentional.
