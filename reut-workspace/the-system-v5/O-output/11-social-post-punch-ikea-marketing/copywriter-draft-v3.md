## Copy Delivery: LinkedIn Post v3

**Channel:** LinkedIn
**Goal:** External Brand Story - IKEA RTM + psychological branding angle. Using the Nike/Joe Whale viral post structure.

---

### Structural Breakdown: Nike/Joe Whale Post (Reference)

1. **Hook:** Outcome-driven provocative question ("How a kid who got scolded... landed a contract with Nike")
2. **Emotional anchor:** "If this isn't real inspiration, I don't know what is."
3. **"Here's the story:"** transition
4. **The underdog setup:** Kid gets in trouble. System says he's wrong. (Empathy engine)
5. **The bold move:** Parents backed him instead of the system.
6. **The proof cascade:** Instagram > 166K followers > Nike contract. Each step escalates.
7. **Brand alignment:** "Perfect match for their brand DNA."
8. **The psychological pivot:** "Here's the part every [audience] should hear:" - shifts from story to MEANING.
9. **The emotional insight:** What validation does to a human being.
10. **The counter-flip:** "Now flip it: What if they had [done the opposite]?" Forces reader to feel the alternative.
11. **The landing:** "They didn't." (2 words. Punch line.)
12. **The reframe:** "That's why this isn't just a [surface story]. It's a masterclass in [deeper lesson]."
13. **Clean close:** "Inspiration, pure and simple."

---

### The Copy

How did a rejected baby monkey turn a $20 toy into one of the biggest brand moments of 2026?

If this isn't a masterclass in real-time marketing, I don't know what is.

Here's the story:

A 7-month-old macaque named Punch was abandoned by his mother at a zoo in Japan.

Zookeepers gave him a $20 IKEA plush orangutan as a comfort toy. He grabbed it and wouldn't let go.

The internet couldn't look away. 30M+ views on TikTok. Global news coverage. Unprecedented crowds at the zoo.

But IKEA didn't just sit back and watch the moment pass. They moved.

IKEA Japan's CEO personally visited the zoo and donated dozens of plushies.

IKEA Spain repurposed Punch's image into a catalog-style ad.

IKEA Hong Kong posted: "You can't adopt him. Buy this IKEA one instead."

And the line that tied it all together: "Sometimes family is who we find along the way."

No agency brief. No campaign timeline. Just a brand that recognized a moment and moved before anyone else could.

Here's the part most founders and marketers should hear:

This didn't go viral because of a monkey. It went viral because of a feeling.

An abandoned baby clinging to the only thing that gives him comfort. Every person who watched that video projected their own story onto it. Loneliness. Rejection. Finding something to hold onto.

That's not content. That's a mirror.

Now flip it:

What if IKEA had ignored this? What if they scheduled a meeting, ran it through legal, waited for the next quarter to "evaluate the opportunity"?

Someone else would have owned the moment.

They didn't wait.

And that's why this isn't just a cute animal story. It's a masterclass in emotional branding.

The best brands don't create feelings. They show up when those feelings already exist.
