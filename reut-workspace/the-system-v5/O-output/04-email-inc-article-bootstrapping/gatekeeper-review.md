## What Works Well

- **Structure is excellent for a journalist.** Numbered answers matching his numbered questions. Easy to scan, easy to pull quotes from. A reporter will love this format.
- **Opening line is about him, not Lior.** "Thanks for including AutoDS in this piece - great angle." Nailed the first-sentence rule.
- **Voice is authentic Lior.** "Honestly, I didn't 'decide' to bootstrap. I just didn't know anything else." This sounds like a real person, not a PR team.
- **Specific numbers present.** $30K to $60K, VAT crisis, zero cash in bank. These are quotable details a journalist needs.
- **Pronoun precision is correct.** "I" for personal decisions ("I had a rule with myself"), "we" for company actions ("We were obsessed with the customer").
- **No corporate jargon.** Zero instances of "leverage," "optimize," or AI-sounding language.
- **Punctuation fingerprint correct.** Single dashes throughout, no em dashes, no double dashes.
- **Casual profanity absent.** Correct choice - this is press, not LinkedIn. "BS" would be inappropriate here.
- **Closing offers value.** "Happy to jump on a quick call" - gives the reporter an easy next step without being pushy.

## What Needs Minor Improvement (3 Edits)

1. **Q1 last line is slightly long.** "At that point, I couldn't find a good reason to give away equity to a third party when things were moving this well on their own." Split for punchier rhythm.
   - **Fix:** "At that point, the company was growing fast. I couldn't find a good reason to give away equity."

2. **Q3 "copied it" is too blunt for press.** A journalist might quote "we copied competitors" out of context and it reads poorly.
   - **Fix:** "We studied what our competitors did well - and learned from it." Keeps the honesty, removes the risk.

3. **Q3 missing a scale indicator.** The snowball line is great but the journalist doesn't know how big. Adding one number gives it weight.
   - **Fix:** Add "We've since served over 1M dropshippers" before the snowball line, so the reader understands the scale of the outcome.

## Answering the Copywriter's Questions

- **Tone for a journalist?** Perfect. Casual-professional is exactly right for Inc. Journalists prefer real talk over polished PR speak. "Honestly" as Q1 opener is fine - it signals authenticity, not casualness.
- **Length?** Over 200 words is acceptable here. This is answering 3 detailed interview questions, not a cold email. The journalist needs substance. Each answer is its own scannable block.
- **Fiverr acquisition?** Good call leaving it out. He'll find it in 30 seconds of research. If you mention it unprompted, it reads like a press release. Let him ask.

## Verdict

Strong draft. The 3 edits are minor. Applied directly to final-email.md.

## Loop Notes
- **New content type: journalist Q&A response.** This doesn't fit the existing 6 LinkedIn archetypes. Worth noting as a separate email pattern.
- **Voice translates well to press contexts.** Same directness, same specificity, but without the LinkedIn-specific formatting (no tags, no 👇, no bold headers).
- **"Copied" vs "learned from" - press sensitivity.** When content may be quoted out of context, soften verbs that could be misread. This is a press-only rule, not a voice change.
- **Length exception: multi-question responses.** The 200-word email guideline doesn't apply when answering multiple interview questions. Each answer is essentially its own mini-email.
