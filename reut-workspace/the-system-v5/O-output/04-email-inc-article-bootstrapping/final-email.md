Thanks for including AutoDS in this piece - great angle. Here are my answers:

**1. The Decision to Bootstrap**

Honestly, I didn't "decide" to bootstrap. I just didn't know anything else. I had no idea how fundraising worked, never thought about it. AutoDS started as a tool I built for myself - I was running multiple eCommerce stores and couldn't find software that handled everything I needed. So I built it. It wasn't meant to become a company.

By the time investors started reaching out and I began getting real offers, the company was already profitable. It was growing fast. I couldn't find a good reason to give away equity.

**2. The Hardest Moment**

I had a rule with myself: run the company on zero cash in the bank. Every dollar that came in from customers went straight back into growth. No safety net.

Then I got a tax report that included VAT I didn't even know I was supposed to set aside. For the first time, the company wasn't just at zero - it was in the red. I actually owed money.

What I did: I hired an advisor, paid extra to work closely with him, and together we built a new mentorship product for our customers. The company doubled in one month - from $30K to $60K in revenue. That's what pulled us out.

**3. Growth Without Capital**

We were obsessed with the customer. We studied what our competitors did well - and learned from it. But more importantly, we studied what they did wrong - and made it amazing. We read every review, every Facebook community thread, every Reddit post. Anything that could tell us what to build next for maximum word-of-mouth impact.

Guerrilla marketing is essential when you don't have the capital for paid acquisition.

What happened: as we kept building exactly what customers needed, people started sharing about us in communities. Mentors started recommending us. Influencers began publishing videos and integrating our tool into their courses. We've since served over 1M dropshippers. From there, it snowballed.
