## Diagnosis: What's Wrong With the Original

**16 problems identified against voice-dna.md and owner editing rules:**

1. **Hook is a question** - Lior's hooks are statements, not questions. "What will happen once you start..." is generic LinkedIn.
2. **"user experience, pricing, and product requirements"** - corporate/abstract language. Lior uses concrete, visual specifics.
3. **"I'm proposing a new perspective"** - formal, coach-mode. Lior doesn't "propose." He says it.
4. **"A product with a roadmap, quarterly OKRs, and clear KPIs"** - jargon stacking. Sounds like a consulting deck.
5. **Bullet list with asterisks for motivational lines** - "You build your own story" / "You become impossible to ignore" - these are fluff. Lior never writes vague motivational bullets.
6. **"I've scaled AutoDS to over 1M users"** - self-promotion without story. Lior shows through scenes, doesn't flex through stats without context.
7. **Bold headers on numbered list items** - owner editing rule: no bold on list headers, inline flow.
8. **"Debug the root cause, don't just patch the symptom"** - forced tech metaphor. One is fine. Three in a row ("debug," "patch," "upgrade your hardware") feels gimmicky.
9. **"Upgrade your hardware"** - body = hardware is a stretch metaphor that doesn't land naturally.
10. **Closing paragraph stacks 4 tech metaphors** - "Debug your schedule. Patch your sleep. Upgrade your hardware. Years of tech optimization..." - too cute, loses authenticity.
11. **No specific story or scene** - the post is entirely abstract. Zero concrete moments, zero "I was in a room and..." energy.
12. **No emotional texture** - no vulnerability, no empathy, no restraint. Just advice.
13. **"Become impossible to ignore"** - vague superlative fluff. On the avoid list.
14. **"Life Sprint"** - branded concept that isn't explained or earned. Lior doesn't drop branded terms.
15. **Too long without earning it** - framework posts can be long, but this has no story to justify the length.
16. **No closing question or clean landing** - just a mic-drop metaphor that doesn't feel real.

## The Rewrite

You spend weeks on your product roadmap. You obsess over every feature, every pricing model, every user flow.
But when was the last time you put that energy into yourself?
Most founders I know treat their personal growth like a nice-to-have. Something they'll get to "when things calm down."
Things never calm down.
I run three companies. I work out 5 days a week, read consistently, and still push myself to learn new skills. Not because I have more time than you. Because I treat myself like I treat my product.
Here's what that looks like in practice:
1. I roadmap my growth. Every quarter I set personal goals the same way I set product OKRs. What skill am I building? What habit am I fixing? If it's not on my calendar, it doesn't exist.
2. I debug myself first. When my focus drops or decisions slow down, I don't push harder. I check the basics. Sleep. Food. Exercise. Most performance problems aren't motivation problems. They're infrastructure problems.
3. I delegate ruthlessly. If someone can do it 70% as well as me, they're doing it. I stopped editing decks, reviewing minor expenses, and scheduling my own meetings a long time ago. That freed up hours I now spend on things only I can do.
You'd never ship a product without a roadmap, testing, and iteration.
Why are you running yourself without one?

## Copywriter Notes

### Choices I Made

**Archetype:** Empathy-first framework (matches gold standard Sample 07)

**Hook:** "You spend weeks on your product roadmap." - Statement, not question. Validates what the reader already does, then pivots to the gap. Same technique as "You love tech. You really do."

**Transition:** "Here's what that looks like in practice:" - peer sharing, not lecturing. No "I'm proposing a new perspective."

**Numbers:** 3 companies, 5 days a week, 70% delegation rule, quarterly cadence - all specific.

**Story/Proof:** "I stopped editing decks, reviewing minor expenses, and scheduling my own meetings a long time ago." - concrete, visual, specific actions (not abstract "delegate more").

**Removed:**
- All stacked tech metaphors ("debug your schedule, patch your sleep, upgrade your hardware")
- "Life Sprint" branded term
- Motivational bullet fluff ("become impossible to ignore")
- "I'm proposing a new perspective"
- Bold headers on numbered list items
- "KPIs" and jargon stacking

**Kept from original (good ideas):**
- "You are the product" core metaphor (strong, just needs better execution)
- 70% delegation rule (practical, specific)
- Debug/check basics concept (used once, not stacked)
- Product roadmap parallel (used as a grounding, not gimmick)

**Ending:** Clean question that creates real tension: "Why are you running yourself without one?" - rhetorical, direct, not generic. The reader has to sit with it.

**Length:** ~220 words. Tight.

### Alternatives Considered

**Alt ending:** "The best product you'll ever build isn't your software. It's yourself." - Decided against it: this is exactly the kind of forced mic-drop the owner cuts from drafts.

**Alt hook:** "I manage myself like I manage my product." - Too self-focused. Starting with "you" is more engaging.
