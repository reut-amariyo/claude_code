# Social Post: AI Fatigue

**Date:** 2026-02-12
**Version:** v2
**Platform:** LinkedIn

---

## The Post

You love tech. You really do.

But somewhere between the 4th AI tool launch this week and your actual to-do list, you stopped keeping up.

You're not alone.

Most of the people I talk to feel the same way. They're smart, they're curious, they genuinely care about staying ahead. But between running a business, managing people, and actually getting work done, there's just no bandwidth left to evaluate every new tool that drops.

So they do one of two things: try everything and burn out, or ignore everything and fall behind.

I've been there. Here's what I changed to stay sharp without drowning in noise:

1. **Start from the need, not the tool.**
Don't ask "what AI tools are out there." Ask "what's the one thing slowing me down the most right now?" Find the pain first. Then go look for a solution. If you start from the tool, you'll end up solving problems you don't have.

2. **Pick 2-3 people you trust. Ignore the rest.**
I follow a handful of people who actually test what they recommend. That's it. No more scrolling through "Top 50 AI Tools" lists at midnight. Find your 2-3 trusted sources and cut everything else out.

3. **Stop chasing. Start reviewing.**
We automated one reporting workflow 8 months ago. Saved us 6 hours a week. Last month, a "better" tool launched. Did I switch? No. Because the tool that works is always better than the tool that's trending. Once a quarter, I review what I use and decide if anything needs to change. That's it.

You don't need to try every tool. You need a system for deciding which ones are worth your time.

The best technology decision I ever made wasn't picking the right tool.

It was learning when to ignore the new ones.

## Copywriter Notes

### Changes in v2 (Based on Owner Feedback)

**Hook rewrite:** Changed from statement ("Every day there's a new AI tool") to empathy-first: "You love tech. You really do." — directly addresses the reader's identity before hitting the pain. They see themselves as tech-savvy. The gap between who they think they are and what they're actually able to do is the real tension.

**Removed "team" framing:** v1 was about "your team" — owner flagged that not everyone has a team. Reframed to the READER being exhausted. "You stopped keeping up" is personal, not organizational.

**Replaced "Here's the 3-step framework I use":** Owner called this boring. New version: "Here's what I changed to stay sharp without drowning in noise" — positions Lior as someone who faced this problem and solved it, not someone handing down a framework from above. CEO sharing, not coaching.

**Removed "project managers":** v1 addressed project managers specifically. Owner wants to address everyone. New version uses "you" throughout. The only role mentioned is generic ("running a business, managing people").

**New ending:** Replaced binary question with a conviction statement: "The best technology decision I ever made wasn't picking the right tool. It was learning when to ignore the new ones." — This is a Reframe ending (Lior's signature). Punchier, more quotable, more shareable than a question.

**Emotional texture:** Added the empathy-before-lesson pattern. "You love tech. You really do." validates the reader first. Then "you stopped keeping up" hits the pain. Then "You're not alone" provides comfort. Then the framework provides the solution.

**Length:** ~250 words — still within LinkedIn sweet spot.

### Questions for the Gatekeeper
1. Is the new hook strong enough? "You love tech. You really do." — empathetic but is it punchy enough for Lior?
2. The ending is a declaration, not a question. Does this still drive engagement or should we add a question?
3. "Once a quarter, I review what I use" — is this specific enough or should we name the review process?
