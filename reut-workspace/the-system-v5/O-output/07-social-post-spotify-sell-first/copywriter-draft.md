# Social Post: Spotify - Sell First, Build Second

**Date:** 2026-02-17
**Version:** v1
**Platform:** LinkedIn

---

## The Post

Spotify's co-CEO just said their top developers haven't written a single line of code since December.

They shipped 50+ features. Served 751 million users. All through AI.

Everyone is talking about the tech.

I want to talk about the pattern behind it.

7 years ago, we launched our Research tool at AutoDS. There was no code. Zero automation. We had a manual team behind the scenes finding products and delivering them by hand.

We sold the vision first. When the first users paid - with their credit cards, not their compliments - we knew it was real.

Only then did we build the tech.

Spotify didn't start with AI either. They started with engineers who understood what needed to be built. The AI just made it faster.

Here's what most founders get wrong: they build first and sell second. Months of development. Perfect features. Beautiful UI.

Then they launch to silence.

Sell the vision until the market literally forces you to build the solution. If they aren't trying to hand you money for a product that doesn't exist yet, don't build it.

What are you building right now that you haven't sold yet? 👇

## Copywriter Notes

### Choices I Made
- **Hook:** Used "Spotify's co-CEO just said..." as a scene-setting authority hook instead of stat-stacking (previous post 06 already used stat-stacking). This puts a real person making a bold claim front and center.
- **Angle shift:** Previous post (06) was "Vision Before Code" - focused on the Spotify tech story with AutoDS as parallel. This version flips it: the LESSON is the headline ("Sell First, Build Second"), Spotify is just the evidence, and AutoDS is the proof.
- **Structure:** External case study + Personal parallel + Framework (the "sell first" principle). Hybrid archetype.
- **"Everyone is talking about the tech. I want to talk about the pattern behind it."** This is a Reframe sentence - redirects from the obvious (AI) to the insight (validation pattern). Positions Lior as someone who sees deeper.
- **"with their credit cards, not their compliments"** - Reframe pattern. Concrete, memorable. Distinguishes real validation from fake signals.
- **"Then they launch to silence."** - Punch Line pattern. Short, after build-up.
- **Conditional Conviction at the end:** "If they aren't trying to hand you money..." - directly from Lior's own words in sample 03. Situational, not preachy.
- **Closing question:** Specific and actionable ("What are you building right now that you haven't sold yet?") rather than generic "What do you think?"
- **Time anchoring:** "7 years ago" for AutoDS story, "since December" for Spotify.
- **Pronouns:** "we" for AutoDS decisions, "they" for Spotify, "you" for reader.

### Alternatives I Considered
- **Contrarian hook:** "Everyone's celebrating that Spotify engineers don't write code anymore. They're missing the point." - Decided against it because the reframe mid-post ("I want to talk about the pattern behind it") is stronger than leading with disagreement.
- **Opening with the AutoDS story first:** Considered starting personal ("7 years ago, we launched...") then connecting to Spotify. But the Spotify news is the trending hook - better to ride the wave then pivot.
- **Ending with "That's it."** - Considered a clean landing. But the topic naturally invites audience reflection, so a specific question works better.
- **3-step framework ending:** Could have added "1. Sell the vision 2. Validate with revenue 3. Build the tech." Decided against it because the post is narrative, not a how-to. Adding steps would break the flow.

### Questions for the Gatekeeper
- Is "Everyone is talking about the tech. I want to talk about the pattern behind it." too close to "But the tech is never the real story" from post 06?
- Does the "Here's what most founders get wrong" paragraph feel condescending? Or does the follow-up detail (months of development, perfect features) make it observational rather than preachy?
- Character count is ~1,100. Within Story + Lesson sweet spot (1,200-1,600) or slightly under. Should I expand, or is tighter better?
