How to steal millions in exposure without being an official sponsor?

[WHOOP](https://www.linkedin.com/company/whoop/) just gave a masterclass in guerrilla marketing.

Here's the story:


Men’s World No. 1 tennis player Carlos Alcaraz stepped onto the court, but before he could hit a single ball, the umpire stopped him.
  

The reason? A tiny strap on his wrist. A WHOOP.

Using trackers during matches is not allowed.

This wasn't a mistake- WHOOP knew the rules.

It was a perfectly engineered marketing move designed to make them the talk of the world.

But WHOOP didn't stop there.
  

With eyes on Whoop, [Will Ahmed](https://www.linkedin.com/in/willahmed/), the CEO, used the opportunity by sending the world's best tennis stars Whoop’s discreet ‘Body’ collection; high-performance apparel with integrated pockets designed to hide the sensor in underwear, boxers, and bras.

  

Their message was clear:

“If you can't wear it on your wrists, hide it in your shorts.”

  

When you build a real brand, the product itself becomes secondary.

WHOOP proved they’re fighting for the athlete’s right to own their health, and that mission drives more sales than any feature ever could.
